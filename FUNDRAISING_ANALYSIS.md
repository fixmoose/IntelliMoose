## LLC vs. Delaware C-Corp: Strategic Decision

### You Asked: "We also need to open a LLC or something, right?"

**Recommended Structure: Delaware C-Corp**

### Why Delaware C-Corp (NOT LLC)?

**1. Investor Expectations**
- 99% of VC-backed startups are Delaware C-Corps
- Investors will not invest in LLCs (tax complications, no preferred stock)
- SAFEs and priced equity rounds require C-Corp structure

**2. Equity Flexibility**
- C-Corp can issue common stock, preferred stock, stock options
- LLC only has "membership interests" (not compatible with standard investor terms)
- 409A valuations and employee stock options require C-Corp

**3. Exit Optionality**
- Acquisitions and IPOs require C-Corp
- Converting LLC â†’ C-Corp later is expensive and tax-inefficient

**4. Credibility**
- "IntelliMoose Inc." sounds more professional than "IntelliMoose LLC"
- Enterprise customers, government contracts, and partners expect C-Corp

### Delaware vs. Other States?

**Why Delaware (Not California, Nevada, Wyoming):**
- 60%+ of Fortune 500 companies are Delaware corporations
- Delaware Chancery Court (specialized business law court)
- Investor-friendly corporate law (well-established case precedents)
- Anonymous beneficial ownership (privacy for founder)
- Low franchise tax for early-stage ($400-1,000/year)

**Cost to Incorporate in Delaware: ~$30K (included in $400K legal budget)**

## How This Protects You Legally

### 1. **No Securities Fraud Liability**

By clearly disclosing:
- All material risks
- Speculative nature of projections
- No guarantees of success

**You cannot be sued for securities fraud** if:
- Investor loses money, or
- Projections don't materialize

**Legal Standard (Rule 10b-5):**
> Fraud requires: (1) Material misrepresentation, (2) Intent to deceive, (3) Reliance by investor

**Your Disclosures Defeat This:**
- No misrepresentation (you disclosed all risks)
- No intent to deceive (clear disclaimers)
- Investor cannot claim reliance (they acknowledged risks)

### 2. **No Negligence Liability**

**"As-Is" Disclaimer + Limitation of Liability:**
> "Investor's sole remedy is the value of their equity stake (which may be zero)."

This limits damages to the value of the investment itself (not consequential damages, lost opportunity costs, etc.)

### 3. **No Fiduciary Duty Before Investment**

**Important:** You do NOT owe fiduciary duties to potential investors.

Only after they invest (and if you control their money as a director) do fiduciary duties arise.

**What You Owe:**
- Duty of loyalty (act in company's best interest)
- Duty of care (make informed decisions)
- Duty of disclosure (share material information)

**What You DON'T Owe:**
- Guarantee of returns
- Obligation to succeed
- Liability for honest mistakes

### 4. **Business Judgment Rule Protection**

**Delaware corporate law protects directors** who:
- Act in good faith
- Make informed decisions (not reckless)
- Believe they're acting in company's best interest

**This Shields You From:**
- Lawsuits over pivots (e.g., focusing on HomeSentinel instead of Trades)
- Failed product launches
- Hiring mistakes
- Strategic errors (as long as not self-dealing or fraud)

### 5. **D&O Insurance Backstop**

**Included in $400K legal budget: $15K/year for D&O insurance**

**What It Covers:**
- Legal defense costs (even if you win)
- Settlements (up to policy limits, typically $1-5M)
- Derivative lawsuits (shareholder sues on company's behalf)

**What It Doesn't Cover:**
- Fraud (intentional misconduct)
- Self-dealing (stealing from company)
- Criminal acts
